You Gotta Know These Piano Sonatas

  • Ludwig van Beethoven, Piano Sonata No. 14 in C-sharp minor (Op. 27 No. 2), “Moonlight”: Marked “quasi una fantasia,” or “almost like a fantasy,” and dedicated to his pupil Giulietta Guicciardi, Ludwig van Beethoven’s Piano Sonata No. 14 was nicknamed “Moonlight” decades after its composition by critic Ludwig Rellstab due to its perceived evocation of Lake Lucerne at nighttime. The first movement, marked Adagio sostenuto, opens with the right hand playing slow triplets outlining a second-inversion C-sharp minor triad; this first movement became so popular that Beethoven reportedly complained to his student Carl Czerny, “Surely I’ve written better things.” The second movement, a D-flat major Allegretto minuet in 3/4 time, was dubbed a “flower between two chasms” by Franz Liszt. The Presto agitato third movement was highly regarded by many; according to Ernst Oster, Frederic Chopin was inspired by the stormy C-sharp minor arpeggios that open the movement to write his compositionally similar Fantaisie-Impromptu.
  • Ludwig van Beethoven, Piano Sonata No. 29 in B-flat Major (Op. 106), “Hammerklavier”: Dedicated to Archduke Rudolph, Beethoven’s Piano Sonata No. 29—published under the title “Grosse Sonate für das Hammer-Klavier,” leading to its nickname—was allegedly described by Beethoven himself as “a hard nut to crack.” It was long thought unplayable until 1836, when Franz Liszt performed it to an enthusiastic review by Hector Berlioz. The sonata’s Allegro first movement, which opens with a series of fortissimo B-flat major chords, bears an impossibly fast tempo marking of 138 BPM on the half note. The second-movement scherzo, marked Assai vivace, includes a trio that interpolates the “Eroica” Symphony’s main theme. The Adagio sostenuto third movement in F-sharp minor has been referred to as a “mausoleum of collective sorrow” due to its slow and solemn pace. The piece’s fourth movement then opens by modulating from the third movement’s key, with quasi-improvisatorial ascending octaves of unharmonized F’s transitioning to a notoriously complex B-flat major fugue in three voices centering on a main theme that leaps by a tenth from F to A.
  • Ludwig van Beethoven, Piano Sonata No. 8 in C minor (Op. 13), “Pathétique”: Dedicated to Prince Karl von Lichnowsky, Beethoven’s Piano Sonata No. 8 in C minor opens with a first movement that alternates between a slow Grave theme and a livelier Allegro con brio section. The A-flat major second movement, marked Adagio cantabile, begins with a theme consisting of the notes C, B-flat, E-flat, D-flat, C played while the right hand crosses over into the bass clef to play a series of slow, undulating sixteenth notes. The third movement, a Rondo allegro, draws material from the preceding two. The second movement is arguably the sonata’s most influential; Edward Elgar claimed he “hinted” at it in the “Nimrod” movement of his Enigma Variations.
  • Ludwig van Beethoven, Piano Sonata No. 23 in F minor (Op. 57), “Appassionata”: Dedicated to Count Franz Brunswick, Beethoven’s Piano Sonata No. 23 in F minor opens its Allegro assai first movement with both hands playing descending and ascending dotted F-minor arpeggios in 12/8 time before repeating the theme a semitone higher. The Andante con moto second movement is a set of variations in D-flat major that ends with a pianissimo deceptive cadence before a sudden fortissimo diminished seventh chord that proceeds attacca into the sonata’s Allegro ma non troppo third movement, which itself climaxes with a coda marked Presto. Beethoven himself titled the piece “La Pasionata”; the modern nickname comes from a later arrangement of the work. Prior to composing the Hammerklavier Sonata, Beethoven considered this to be his most difficult sonata.
  • Ludwig van Beethoven, Piano Sonata No. 18 in E-flat Major (Op. 31, No. 3), “The Hunt”: As with many Beethoven works, the nickname “The Hunt” did not come from Beethoven itself; it came from a listener who felt a theme in the work’s finale resembled a horn call used during hunting. Unusually for a piano sonata, this work has four movements (rather than three) and lacks a slow movement. The Allegro first movement, which opens with a recurring dotted-eighth sixteenth figure, contains a chord that is harmonically equivalent to Richard Wagner’s Tristan chord (though Beethoven voices it differently). The sonata’s second-movement Scherzo is also atypical, as it is in double time instead of triple time and sonata form as opposed to ternary form. Camille Saint-Saëns based his two-piano Variations on Theme of Beethoven on material from the third-movement Minuet.
  • Ludwig van Beethoven, Piano Sonata No. 26 in E-flat Major (Op. 81a), “Les Adieux”: Beethoven’s Piano Sonata No. 26—whose title means “The Farewell” in English—is a programmatic work written after Beethoven’s patron Archduke Rudolphe fled Vienna in the wake of a Napoleonic invasion. The three chords that open the first movement’s introductory Adagio are written beneath the syllables of the word “lebewohl” (German for “farewell”). The second and third movements are titled, respectively, “Abwesenheit” (“Absence”) and “Das Wiedersehen” (“The Return”), with the latter having a German tempo instruction translating to “in the liveliest of time.” Beethoven’s work was likely inspired, at least in part, by Czech composer Jan Dussek’s piano sonata of the same name, published a decade before Beethoven wrote his.
  • Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart, Piano Sonata No. 11 in A Major, K. 331: Although the first two movements of Mozart’s Piano Sonata No. 11—an Andante grazioso theme and variations and a minuet, respectively—are still occasionally performed today, it is the third movement Rondo alla turca, or “Turkish March,” that has become one of the most ubiquitous pieces of classical music in popular culture. The Allegretto movement, which opens with a falling and rising sixteenth note motive in A minor, was intended by Mozart to evoke the sound of the janissary bands from the Ottoman empire, a type of early marching band that featured woodwinds, brass, drums, and cymbals; the numerous rolls in the piano’s lower registers bring to mind drumrolls, and the high, bright octaves in the right hand the blaring of trumpets.
  • Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart, Piano Sonata No. 16 in C Major, K. 545, “Simple”: Also known as the “Semplice” or “Facile” sonata, Mozart’s Piano Sonata No. 16 in C major is a common piece learned by piano students, as per Mozart’s stated intention of it being “for beginners.” The piece’s most recognizable motif comes at the beginning of its Allegro first movement, with the right hand playing the ascending notes C, E, G, then going back down to B, C, D, C. This opening theme is accompanied by a left-hand Alberti bass figure outlining a major triad. The piece’s ubiquity inspired Edvard Grieg to compose an original second piano part to be played in duet form with the original sonata.
  • Frédéric Chopin, Piano Sonata No. 2 in B-flat minor (Op. 35): Written while living in George Sand’s manor in the village of Nohant, Chopin’s Piano Sonata No. 2 in B-flat minor opens with a Grave first movement before transitioning to a doppio movimento section that is often erroneously repeated due to an engraving error in the Leipzig edition. The sonata’s best-known portion is the third-movement “Funeral March,” an oft-excerpted and -arranged work whose opening motive of repeated B-flats has become ubiquitously associated with death (and leading to the work as a whole sometimes being nicknamed the “Funeral March”). The work’s fourth and final movement, which is marked Presto and played in 2/2 time, is composed of furious chromatic flurries of eighth notes before the final bar indicates that the pedal should be pressed to give the impression of “wind washing over graves.”
  • Franz Liszt, Piano Sonata in B minor, S. 178: Unusually for a pianist as renowned as Liszt, the Sonata in B minor (which Liszt dedicated to Robert Schumann) was premiered not by Liszt himself, but by Hans von Bülow. This sonata is not formally split into movements, moving between its distinct sections without pause; that said, most scholars concur that the sonata has four clear sections, often likened to the movements of a symphony. The piece is an example of double-function form, with the four sections serving as both their own distinct segments and as subdivisions of a single, large unifying sonata form. Critically panned when it was premiered, Liszt’s sonata has since become a cornerstone of the piano repertoire. British composer Humphrey Searle (whose last name is the “S.” in the catalogue numbers of Liszt’s works) orchestrated the work, which became the basis for Frederick Ashton’s ballet Marguerite and Armand.

This article was contributed by NAQT writer Wonyoung Jang.
