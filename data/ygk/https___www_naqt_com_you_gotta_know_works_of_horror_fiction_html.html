You Gotta Know These Works of Horror Fiction

  • “The Fall of the House of Usher” by Edgar Allan Poe (1839) Poe wrote “The Fall of the House of Usher” in the style of Gothic literature, which centers on atmospheres of dread and haunting; “Usher” contains extreme emphases on the supernatural and macabre. In the story, the unnamed narrator visits Roderick Usher—a sick old friend—at his crumbling lakeside mansion, which Roderick thinks may be alive. Roderick’s sister Madeline is also ill and eventually succumbs and is interred in the Ushers’ crypt. In the coming days, Roderick and the narrator hear strange noises. During a storm, Roderick screams that the lake outside is glowing and the two hear more noises before being attacked by Madeline, who had been mistakenly buried alive (a frequent trope in Poe’s stories). Roderick dies of fright and Madeline drops dead; the narrator flees the house as it cracks in two and sinks into the waters.
  • “The Yellow Wallpaper” by Charlotte Perkins Gilman (1892) Inspired by Gilman’s own bouts of depression, “The Yellow Wallpaper” chronicles a young wife undergoing a controversial, real-life therapy called a “rest cure” for her mental illness. This requires her to be kept in her bed in a room in an isolated house where all mental stimuli are forbidden. She becomes fixated on the intricate yellow wallpaper in her room; as her husband continues confining her, she begins to believe there is a shadowy woman inside the wallpaper who must be freed, and she starts compulsively peeling it. The story’s tone begins calm, but by the end—when the woman’s husband finally unlocks the door and faints upon seeing what the narrator has become—the narration vividly devolves into near-incoherent ravings. “The Yellow Wallpaper” has been extensively analyzed by feminist critics for its themes of being literally “trapped” by one’s husband.
  • Dracula by Bram Stoker (1897) Vampire fiction had existed in the century prior to Dracula (most notably John William Polidori’s The Vampyre and Sheridan Le Fanu’s Carmilla), but Stoker’s Dracula was such a success that it codified almost all recognizable modern vampire tropes—including sleeping in a coffin of dirt, a lack of reflection, shapeshifting abilities, and needing an invitation to cross a threshold—all of which Stoker invented. Stoker took the name Dracula from Dracul, the family name of Vlad the Impaler, a historical Romanian ruler noted for his extreme cruelty toward his enemies. The story is epistolary (told via characters’ journals and letters) and begins with Jonathan Harker being sent to Transylvania to help a count named Dracula buy homes in England. Dracula sails to England and soon preys upon Lucy Westenra, who in turn preys on innocents and children. Harker, his Texan friend Quincey Morris, and the academic Abraham Van Helsing eventually deduce Dracula’s true nature and kill him by slashing his throat and driving Morris’s bowie knife through his heart.
  • The Turn of the Screw by Henry James (1898) Commissioned explicitly as a ghost story for Collier’s Weekly, the novella The Turn of the Screw follows a governess hired to raise Miles and Flora, two children who live in a mansion in rural England. The governess learns that her predecessor Miss Jessel died, as did another servant, Peter Quint, whom Jessel loved. Two unfamiliar figures keep appearing around the estate; the governess worries that they are talking to Miles and Flora and may actually be Jessel and Quint’s ghosts. Miles tells the governess that he cannot recall why he was expelled from school, hinting that he may already have come under Quint’s control. In a climactic scene, the governess sees Quint’s ghost at a window and shields Miles from the phantom, but when it vanishes, Miles has died. The Turn of the Screw is noted for its unreliable narrator, with many critics positing that the ghosts are a product of the governess’ mind. The novel Jane Eyre was a key inspiration for James’s work.
  • “The Monkey’s Paw” by W. W. Jacobs (1902) The White family are on vacation when Morris, a friend and soldier, shows them a mummified monkey paw that he says grants wishes at a great price. The Whites take the paw and wish for money, which manifests as them being compensated when their son dies in a work accident. Their hasty second wish, to restore the son to life, is quickly followed by sudden banging at the front door. Mrs. White, fearing that their son is now the living dead, makes a third wish; when Mr. White opens the door, no one is there. Many folktales have explored un-ideal wishes (Faust and “Aladdin,” in particular), but “The Monkey’s Paw” originated the trope of an ironic twist resulting from a wish being granted in the exact literal sense.
  • “The Call of Cthulhu” by H. P. Lovecraft (1928) This core story in Lovecraft’s “mythos” about the ancient god-level alien Cthulhu is told in three parts. In the first, a young artist dreams of a weird city and sculpts an image of an octopus-dragon-man; in the second, a New Orleans police raid on a cult devoted to the “Great Old Ones” yields a similar statuette; in the third account, a ship stumbles upon an uncharted island city with non-Euclidean geometry where sailors mistakenly awaken and release Cthulhu, a titanic monster (this third, climactic section is a rework of Lovecraft’s early story “Dagon”). As in most of Lovecraft’s fiction, “The Call of Cthulhu” features educated men whose scientific expertise proves ineffective and useless against the universe’s hidden truths—a central element of Lovecraft’s genre of “cosmic horror.”
  • The Haunting of Hill House by Shirley Jackson (1959) Dr. John Montague recruits three companions for a planned paranormal study at Hill House, a supposedly haunted mansion. The team of four experiences many supernatural events, but the protagonist, Eleanor Vance, is the target of the most by far—including ghostly chalk writing that identifies Eleanor by name. Other events, such as touches in the dark, apparitions, and entities trying to open locked doors, are never depicted with total clarity, and Jackson presents many explanations for them, including real ghosts, Eleanor being insane, or even the notion that Eleanor has unknown telekinesis; this ambiguity is universally praised, and has led the novel to be esteemed by horror authors and literary critics alike since its release. By the end of the novel, Eleanor has become certain she “belongs” at Hill House, and when the team forces her to leave, she deliberately drives her car into a tree.
  • Something Wicked This Way Comes by Ray Bradbury (1962) Jim and Will are two teenagers whose town is visited by a carnival run by Mr. Dark, who has an uncanny ability to fulfill people’s desires. The boys learn that the carnival’s carousel can age or de-age whoever rides it, and Mr. Dark and his workers—including the “Dust Witch”—are evil entities who feed on people’s lifeforce. Jim, Will, and Will’s father Charles discover that laughter and smiles weaken the carnival; they use a bullet engraved with a smile to kill the Dust Witch and use the carousel to make Mr. Dark age into a tiny boy who is killed by joyful laughing. The novel is often compared to Bradbury’s non-horror novel Dandelion Wine for its nostalgic depiction of small-town life. Bradbury took the novel’s title from a line spoken by one of the three witches in Shakespeare’s Macbeth.
  • Interview with the Vampire by Anne Rice (1976) Anne Rice’s debut novel spans 200 years in the life of Louis de Pointe du Lac, an American planter who is turned into a vampire by Lestat de Lioncourt (the main character in Rice’s The Vampire Chronicles series). Lestat and Louis have a contentious friendship that is further complicated when Lestat turns the little girl Claudia into a vampire, which keeps her perpetually young as time passes. Their resentment prompts them to try to burn Lestat alive and flee to Europe, where Claudia is killed by a coven of theatre-loving vampires after Lestat (who survived) pursues them to Paris. Louis escapes back to the U.S., where Lestat also now resides in a derelict house in New Orleans. The novel helped solidify several post-Dracula vampire tropes, such as that sunlight incinerates vampires (an element first depicted in the silent film Nosferatu) and that they are immortal and un-aging. The book owed much of its initial and continuing success to gay fans; while Interview with the Vampire does not feature overt gay romance, many LGBT critics identify queer themes of isolation and subtextual homoeroticism in Lestat and Louis’s relationship.
  • It by Stephen King (1986) The title entity of It is an interdimensional creature that terrorizes the small town of Derry, Maine (a recurring locale in King’s novels, along with Maine in general). “It” is a shapeshifter who manifests in many terrifying forms, but most often as the sinister Pennywise the Dancing Clown. The novel is told in two parallel timelines: one in which a group of outcast classmates, “the Losers Club,” are menaced by “It” (as well as by the bigoted bully Henry Bowers); and another timeline 27 years later when “It” returns and the Losers Club reunites to kill “It” in a reality-bending battle of will. The way “It” resurfaces periodically has prompted critics to view “It” as a manifestation of America’s cultural evils, which is supported by several incidents in It of explicitly racial, religious, and homophobic violence. The novel’s two major screen adaptations—a 1990 miniseries starring Tim Curry and a 2010s film duology starring Bill Skarsgård—have entrenched Pennywise the Clown as one the most iconic monsters in all of horror media.

This article was contributed by NAQT editor Danny Kristian Vopava.
