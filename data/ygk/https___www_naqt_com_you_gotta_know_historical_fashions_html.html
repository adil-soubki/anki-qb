You Gotta Know These Historical Fashions

  • The hennin (mid- to late 15th century) The hennin was a tall, roughly conical hat worn by ladies beginning in the mid-15th century. Though it has become emblematic of “fairy tale” costumes, the hennin was very popular in France and (particularly) Burgundy, whose control of Flemish ports and manufacturing hubs brought easy access to luxury fabrics. To a lesser extent, the hennin was also worn in England and in the German domains. The hennin was always worn with fine silk or gauze draping down from its top. Though the long, pointed cone was the most popular style, several variants are widely seen in medieval art and early Renaissance portraiture, including the truncated hennin (with a shape often compared to an inverted flowerpot) and curved, yard-long hennins that resembled elaborate horns (which were denounced by moralists for its diabolical connotations). In all cases, the hennin was worn high and back on the head in conjunction with the enduring fashion of the high plucked hairline.
  • The French hood and gable hood (early to late 16th century) In contrast with the extravagant hennin, the hood was a subtler, smaller head covering for the upper classes that entered fashion in the first decade of the 16th century and remained popular (especially among older ladies) until the very end of that century. Pearls were a common embellishment. There were two main styles: the gable hood, which was popular in England and had a quasi-pentagonal, angled shape that covered most of the head down to the ears; and the French hood, which was more popular in France and curved backward atop the head to expose much of the hair. The French hood later subsumed the gable hood in popularity, even in England. Hoods are extremely common in art from the era, especially in portraits of Henry VIII’s wives and the other Tudor queens.
  • The men’s wig (1650s to 1800s) The wig became a core element of men’s European fashion in the 1620s when France’s Louis XIII went bald young and began wearing wigs, a practice his son Louis XIV continued. Britain resisted this continental fashion until the Restoration, when Charles II (who had been in exile in France) proudly wore wigs at court. These wigs, while massive and wide, originally mimicked natural hair color; by the 1750s, the standard male wig was less elaborate, often had a short ponytail-like queue, and was white to better match the white powder used to maintain it. The male wig fell out of fashion in the 1800s for two chief reasons: the first was a severe English tax on wig powder passed in 1795; the second was that wigs, a symbol of royalty, became distasteful amidst the rise of populist republics in America and France. (This, however, did not stop early American presidents such as John Adams and James Monroe from wearing the wigs that had been fashionable when they were younger.)
  • The kilt (mid-17th century onward) Contrary to common depictions in film and television, the Scottish kilt did not exist in the medieval era. The great kilt (an immense garment that wraps around the waist and is then wrapped up around the shoulder) came about first, with the more recognizable small kilt (waist-only) arising in the early 18th century. The practice of wearing the kilt in Scotland caught on immediately, and it became so quickly and deeply tied to Scottish identity that, after Britain crushed the nationalist Jacobite Rising of 1745, the kilt was banned under the Dress Act for four decades. The kilt was revived in the 1820s when the fashion-obsessed George IV wore one in public, and it became heavily romanticized. It was not until this later 19th-century revival that clan-specific tartan patterns developed and myths arose regarding who was “allowed” to wear certain tartans.
  • Crinoline and hoop skirts (mid-19th century) Structured ladies’ skirts had always been around to some extent (such as the wide-hipped dresses and panniers of the Rococo era), but in the 1840s, European women began layering underskirt upon underskirt beneath dresses to create a slim-waisted, bell-shaped silhouette called “crinoline” (after crin, a common fabric in the underskirts). Crinoline exploded in popularity in 1856 with the patenting of cage crinoline, a rigid, light, lattice-like underskirt; this did away with the heavy multi-layering method, allowing crinoline to be affordable for all social classes and resulting in super-wide hoop skirts. Crinoline could sometimes prove hazardous: hundreds of women died from their hoop skirts draping into fireplaces and catching fire. Crinoline is now emblematic of the early Victorian era (and of Antebellum society in the U.S.). In the 1870s crinoline and hoop skirts faded in popularity but lived on as the vastly minimized bustle, which extended only from the backside.
  • The Victorian corset (mid-19th century to early 20th century) Like structured skirts, corsets in some form were part of women’s dress for centuries, but beginning in the 1820s, tight-laced corsets became near-compulsory in women’s fashion. By the mid-19th century, after crinoline fell from fashion, the “wasp waist” was seen as ideal (though the oft-cited ideal of the 18″ waistline may be an exaggeration or myth), and it was achieved through very tight cinching of whalebone corsets. From its outset, tight-laced corsetry was known to be unhealthy, and many newspapers of the time ran articles denouncing its excesses. Still, the corset remained ubiquitous at the turn of the century, when it was core to the all-American “Gibson girl” ideal. It was only the wholesale social changes brought on by World War I that caused the corset to vanish from everyday dress.
  • Reform dress (mid-19th century) The controversy over corsets coincided with the first wave of feminism in Europe, and many activists proposed alternatives that were collectively called “reform dress.” In the 1850s, Amelia Jenks Bloomer, an American feminist, advocated for a loose feminine trouser suit known by her name—“bloomers” or the “bloomer suit”—but constant ridicule led to its abandonment. In that same decade, “Artistic Dress” arose among followers of the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood, who favored looser, medieval-inspired garb. Reform dress was typically viewed as extreme, as it played into growing worries about women’s emancipation thanks to industrialization and the bicycle. Ultimately, outside of the emancipation waist (a bodice undergarment of strong fabric), no element of “reform dress” achieved anything more than fleeting use outside activist circles.
  • Flapper fashion (1910s–1930) The term “flapper” originally referred to a vivacious teenage girl, but by the late 1910s it came to identify impetuous young women who flouted long-standing gender norms. In particular, the “flapper” wore short skirts (above the knee), cut her hair short into a bob, and enjoyed conventionally masculine pastimes, which at the time were as innocuous as driving a car, smoking, and drinking hard liquor. The aesthetics of jazz clubs also played into the flapper image, as did contemporary mainstream fashion items such as cloche hats (bell-shaped hats that cup close to the face) and heavy use of rouge, eyeliner, and lipstick. Though it had analogs in Europe and the U.K., the flapper was chiefly an American phenomenon closely tied to the prosperity of the Roaring Twenties; its appeal evaporated amidst the austerity of the Great Depression.
  • The bikini (1946 to today) The bikini debuted at a time when women’s swimsuits were usually a generous top paired with high-rise shorts that bared some midriff. Designed by Louis Reard for a swimsuit fashion show in Paris in 1946, the bikini was an instant controversy, as it was little more than string briefs paired with a bra. Reard named the garment after the Bikini Atoll, where the U.S. held atomic bomb tests less than a week prior. The bikini was a hit at the Paris show but proved too shocking to mainstream culture. In the 1950s, it was often worn by movie stars such as Marilyn Monroe or fashion models as publicity stunts; it was not until 1962, when Ursula Andress wore an iconic white bikini in a scene in the first James Bond film, Dr. No, that the bikini gained mainstream acceptance.
