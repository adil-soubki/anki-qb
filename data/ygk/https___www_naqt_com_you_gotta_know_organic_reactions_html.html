You Gotta Know These Organic Reactions

This article uses standard organic chemistry terminology by describing molecules in terms of their functional groups. If you do not know what functional groups are, read You Gotta Know These Functional Groups. You may also find it helpful to see diagrams of functional groups.

  • Redox reactions change the oxidation state of a compound by reducing or oxidizing it. A compound that is oxidized loses electrons, while a compound that is reduced gains electrons. In the context of organic molecules, an oxidation reaction tends to reduce the number of carbon–hydrogen bonds and increase the number of bonds between carbon and oxygen or other atoms, such as nitrogen, halogens, etc. Reduction reactions do the opposite, increasing carbon–hydrogen bonds and decreasing carbon–oxygen and carbon–other atom bonds. For example, the conversion of an alkene to an alkane increases the number of carbon–hydrogen bonds and is thus a reduction. Redox reactions can convert between useful functional groups such as alcohols, carbonyls, and carboxylic acid derivatives. Alcohols can be oxidized to carbonyls, which can be oxidized to carboxylic acid derivatives; the reverse order can occur through reduction reactions. Oxidation is frequently performed with chromium-based oxidants such as PCC or Jones’ reagent. Reductions are frequently performed with hydride reducing agents like sodium borohydride (Na B H4) or lithium aluminum hydride (Li Al H4).
  • Addition reactions are a type of reaction in which two molecules combine to produce a larger molecule with no loss of atoms from either of the reactants. Organic addition reactions can be categorized as electrophilic or nucleophilic depending on whether the molecule being added has a group that accepts electron pairs (and thus “loves electrons” and is an electrophile) or if the molecule being added has a group that donates electron pairs to positively-charged nuclei (and thus “loves nuclei” and is a nucleophile). For example, in the electrophilic addition reaction between an alkene and molecular bromine (Br2), bromine acts as an electrophile, accepting electrons from the alkene, the nucleophile. Addition reactions to alkenes may be regioselective, meaning the added substituent may selectively prefer to bond to one of the two alkene carbon atoms over the other carbon atom; such regioselectivity can in many cases be described by Markovnikov’s rule.
  • Substitution reactions are a type of reaction in which one group on a larger molecule is replaced with a different group. The incoming group is usually a nucleophile, and the group that is replaced is called a leaving group. There are two basic types of substitution reactions, denoted SN2 (which stands for “bimolecular nucleophilic substitution”) and SN1 (which stands for “unimolecular nucleophilic substitution”). An SN2 reaction is called “bimolecular” and has a “2” in its abbreviation because its rate-determining step depends on two molecules, with the nucleophile attacking the leaving group via a backside attack. An SN1 reaction is called “unimolecular” and has a “1” in its abbreviation since its rate-determining step depends on one molecule: first, the leaving group leaves by itself spontaneously, forming an unstable carbocation intermediate, then the nucleophile comes in afterward. The Williamson ether synthesis is a typical substitution reaction; in it, an alcohol replaces the halogen in an alkyl halide via an SN2 mechanism, making an ether.
  • Elimination reactions are the opposite of an addition reaction: instead of two molecules coming together to form one molecule, two groups (or, more commonly, one group and a hydrogen atom) are removed from a molecule to form two molecules. The original molecule then usually is stabilized by the creation of a pi bond. This is most commonly observed to create alkenes by eliminating across a carbon–carbon single bond. Just like substitution reactions, basic elimination reactions come in E2 (bimolecular elimination) and E1 (unimolecular elimination) types. Like in SN2 reactions, in E2 reactions the reaction occurs in a single rate-determining step involving both molecules as the leaving group and the adjacent hydrogen leave at the same time. E2 reactions require an anti-periplanar arrangement of the leaving group and the exiting hydrogen to proceed. Like SN1 reactions, in E1 reactions the rate-determining step involves one molecule as the leaving group leaves spontaneously to form an unstable carbocation, then afterwards the hydrogen is removed to form the pi bond. Similar to Markovnikov’s rule for addition reactions, the regioselectivity of some elimination reactions obeys Zaitsev’s rule, in which the leaving hydrogen is removed from the carbon attached to the fewest hydrogens, thus creating the most substituted alkene.
  • Condensation reactions are a type of reaction in which two molecules combine to form a single molecule, with the removal of a small molecule in the process (usually water, hence the name). A classic example of a condensation reaction is the Fischer esterification, in which a carboxylic acid reacts with an alcohol to produce an ester. The Fischer esterification requires an acid catalyst to protonate the carbonyl oxygen in the carboxylic acid. Another condensation reaction is the aldol condensation, in which two carbonyl-containing compounds combine to form an alpha–beta unsaturated ketone, which means a ketone that is directly connected to an alkene. The aldol condensation can be catalyzed by either an acid or a base, with the acid generating an intermediate enol, or the base generating an intermediate enolate.
  • Hydrogenation is a special type of reduction reaction in which a molecule of hydrogen (H2) is added to an organic molecule. It is most typically used to convert alkenes (double bonds) to alkanes (single bonds). This is commonly used in the processing of fats, in which unsaturated fatty acids—which have double and triple bonds—are hydrogenated until they are saturated with hydrogen atoms, thus becoming saturated fats. Incomplete hydrogenation can lead to the production of trans alkenes, thus creating trans fats. William Knowles and Ryoji Noyori won the 2001 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for independently developing stereoselective hydrogenation reactions. This essentially means that the reaction preferentially adds the hydrogens to a particular side of a molecule, forming a chiral molecule, a molecule that cannot be superimposed on its mirror image. (Think about your left hand and right hand: if you move one of them over the other without rotating, the hands don’t overlap cleanly because your thumbs are on opposite sides. Your hands are chiral! These stereoselective reactions are capable of producing either only the left-handed molecule, or only the right-handed molecule.) Stereoselective hydrogenation can be used to make the drug L-DOPA, which is used to treat Parkinson’s disease.
  • The Diels–Alder reaction is a cycloaddition reaction that combines a diene and a dienophile to form a cyclic compound, typically a six-carbon cyclohexene ring. The dienophile is typically an alkene, but can also be another functional group with an appropriate pi bond (such as an alkyne). Due to the cyclic transition state of the Diels–Alder reaction, it belongs to the pericyclic class of reactions. The stereochemistry of a Diels–Alder reaction kinetically has a preference for the endo product, because that configuration maximizes the orbital overlap between the two reactants. Frontier molecular orbital theory can be used to describe the electron flow in a Diels–Alder reaction: electrons flow from the HOMO (highest occupied molecular orbital) of the diene to the LUMO (lowest unoccupied molecular orbital) of the dienophile. This means that the Diels–Alder reaction can be accelerated by adding electron-donating groups (EDGs) to the diene and electron-withdrawing groups (EWGs) to the dienophile.
  • The Wittig reaction converts carbonyls into alkenes. This is typically accomplished with a Wittig reagent, which is a phosphonium ylide, a type of molecule with a positively-charged phosphorus atom directly bonded to a negatively-charged carbon atom. The ylide can be made by taking an alkylated phosphonium salt and treating it with a strong organolithium base (like n-butyllithium). This ylide then reacts with the carbonyl to form a four-membered ring called an oxaphosphetane, which decomposes to give the product alkene, plus triphenylphosphine oxide as a side product. Wittig reactions typically produce (Z) alkenes, meaning the two largest substituents are on the same side of the double bond. The Horner–Wadsworth–Emmons reaction is a modification of the Wittig reaction that employs “stabilized” ylides to instead produce (E) alkenes, in which the two largest substituents are on opposite sides of the double bond.
  • Electrophilic aromatic substitution (EAS) reactions are substitution reactions that can replace groups on an aromatic ring like benzene. These use a different mechanism than SN2 or SN1, as aromatic carbons are difficult to nucleophilically attack. While there are six positions on a benzene ring to add a new substituent, you often only want to add the group to one position, which can be accomplished with the aid of directing groups. For example, the hydroxyl group in the molecule phenol is an ortho/para director, meaning the new group will be added either next to the hydroxyl (ortho), or on the opposite side of the ring to the hydroxyl (para). Nitro groups are meta-directors, which influence the addition of the new substituent two carbon atoms away from the nitro group (meta). The Friedel–Crafts reactions are a prominent example of an electrophilic aromatic substitution, in which an alkyl or acyl group is added to an aromatic ring. Friedel–Crafts reactions typically use a Lewis acid, such as aluminum trichloride, as a catalyst.
  • Coupling reactions combine two molecules or fragments into one larger product, usually with the aid of organometallic reagents to form new carbon–carbon bonds (which are difficult to make, but desirable). The 2010 Nobel Prize in Chemistry was awarded to Akira Suzuki, Richard Heck, and Ei-ichi Negishi for developing coupling reactions named for them (the Suzuki coupling, the Negishi coupling, and the Heck reaction). All coupling reactions have a mechanism that contains a catalytic cycle, allowing for a very small amount of catalyst (usually a palladium complex) to propagate the reaction. Suzuki reactions usually couple a boronic ester to a vinyl or aryl halide, while Heck reactions couple an alkene to a vinyl or aryl halide.
