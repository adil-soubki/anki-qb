You Gotta Know These Computer Components

  • The CPU (central processing unit) is the heart of a computer. The CPU performs all of the binary operations responsible for a computer’s general functions and issues commands to the rest of the hardware. A modern CPU consists of multiple cores (processing units capable of running independently), which can be multithreaded to perform more than one set of calculations simultaneously; a high-end modern processor for a gaming PC may have 8 cores and 16 threads, whereas high-end workstations for video rendering and/or editing may have a CPU with 64 or even 96 cores. Modern CPU manufacturers include Intel and AMD (Advanced Micro Devices), whose chips are based on the 64-bit version of the x86 instruction set. In the early 2020s, Apple began designing its own line of Apple Silicon processors for its computers, based on the ARM instruction set (more common in smartphones and tablets).
  • The motherboard is a large printed circuit board connecting all of the main components of a computer. A typical motherboard will contain, at the very least, a socket for the CPU, multiple slots to insert RAM, slots in which to insert a graphics card and/or other expansion cards, connectors for storage drives, and an I/O panel that is available on the back of the case, which contains plugs for external peripherals, USB devices, networking ports, and more. A motherboard’s chipset manages the communication between a computer’s components, which occurs via the motherboard’s integrated circuits. In the 1990s and early 2000s, it was common for most desktops to have a separate sound card to manage sound output; since the early 2000s, most motherboards have had integrated sound, largely eliminating sound cards except in specialized applications.
  • RAM (random access memory) is a form of memory used to store data a computer expects to need within a short amount of time. Modern RAM comes in sticks called DIMMs (dual inline memory modules). RAM generally stores information based on whether capacitors are charged or uncharged. RAM is volatile memory, meaning that any information stored in it is lost when the computer is shut down or powered off (unlike information stored on a hard drive). Nearly all RAM in modern desktop computers is SDRAM (synchronous dynamic RAM), meaning that an external clock cycle controls the speed at which information is written to and read from the RAM, which must be refreshed at certain intervals. Most modern desktop RAM also uses the DDR (double data rate) standard, meaning that data can be read or written twice within a single clock cycle. The speed of RAM is also partially measured by its CAS (column address strobe) latency, the number of clock cycles between when data is requested and received.
  • Hard drives (HDDs) are non-volatile devices for storing data, meaning that the data is not lost when the computer is turned off. By and large, hard drives still operate on the same basic principles as when they were first developed in the 1950s: data is magnetically written onto a number of fast-spinning metal plates or disks. Hard drives remain the cheapest used storage solution per unit of data (among solutions used commonly for general purposes). Most modern desktop computers will include at least one hard drive for bulk data storage, though it will likely not be the system’s primary drive. Nearly all modern desktop hard drives are of the 3.5″ size, spin at 5400 or 7200 RPM, and connect to the motherboard via a SATA cable.
  • Solid state drives (SSDs) are non-volatile devices that store data on flash memory, which utilizes transistors. Because the drive essentially uses electricity, rather than physically spinning media, to store information, SSD read/write speeds are much faster than those of hard drives, but they are also more expensive: a 2-terabyte SSD may cost around $200, while the same amount of money may purchase an 8-terabyte hard drive. SSDs first began to be widely adopted in the consumer market in the early 2010s. There are currently two main types of SSDs: 2.5″ drives, which connect to the motherboard with a SATA cable (like hard drives); and M.2 NVME drives, which are the size of a stick of gum, connect directly to a slot on the motherboard, and represent the fastest easily available non-volatile consumer storage solution today. Most modern computers will include at least one SSD as the main drive on which the operating system is installed, allowing for much faster boot times than a hard drive.
  • A graphics card, containing a GPU, generates the visual images that are output to a monitor. Nearly all computers designed for gaming will use a discrete graphics card; systems for general business purposes and most non-gaming laptops instead use integrated graphics, meaning the CPU also acts as the GPU. The two main manufacturers of discrete consumer graphics cards are Nvidia (with its GeForce cards) and AMD (with its Radeon cards); beginning in 2022, Intel also began manufacturing discrete GPUs with its Arc line of products. A modern discrete graphics card will include its own processing unit (the actual graphics processing unit, or GPU), as well as several gigabytes of dedicated RAM. Nvidia’s development of CUDA technology in the 2000s allowed for GPU processing power to be used by other applications. Over the past decade, this has led to a spike in GPU prices and numerous shortages of available products, as GPUs have become critical to both cryptocurrency mining and generative AI.
  • The power supply unit (PSU) supplies power to all other components of a computer by converting the alternating current from an outlet into direct current. Power supplies are rated by both how many watts of power they are able to provide a system, and their 80 Plus rating—a measure of the efficiency of their power delivery; for example, an 850 Gold PSU can deliver up to 850 watts of power at between 87 and 90 percent efficiency under load. Modern power supplies for enthusiast desktops are often modular, meaning that the user only needs to connect cables for the components that they need or have—reducing the amount of cable clutter within a case. While power supplies deliver different voltages for different components, by far the most important is the 12-volt rail, as the most power-hungry of a system’s components—the CPU and GPU—use it.
  • PCI Express (Peripheral Component Interface Express or PCIe) is a connection standard used for computer hardware. First developed in 2003, PCIe began life as a new standard for connecting graphics cards that replaced the older, slower AGP (Acceleratic Graphics Port) standard. However, its speed and versatility meant that soon it became the standard for all sorts of components, including sound cards and other expansion cards; modern M.2 NVME drives also utilize PCIe. PCIe operates on a number of lanes for transmitting data, from one to 16; most modern GPU slots utilize a 16-lane connection. Because of PCIe’s ubiquity, it is common for both the CPU and motherboard chipset to include separate PCIe controllers that are linked to different inputs; it is also, consequently, possible to install so many PCIe devices on one controller that it limits the available lanes and reduces performance.
  • The BIOS (Basic Input/Output System) or UEFI (Unified Extensible Firmware Interface) is the first piece of software that runs upon powering on a computer. Although the terms “BIOS” and “UEFI” technically refer to different things (BIOS being an older IBM standard that has been superseded by UEFI), the terms are still commonly used interchangeably. The BIOS performs a computer’s POST (power-on self test), ensuring all hardware components are functioning, and manages the boot order, determining which drive/operating system will be given priority to run. Updating a computer’s BIOS (known as flashing the BIOS) was once a process fraught with danger, as if the computer was turned off or otherwise lost power, the BIOS could be corrupted and render the system completely unusable; most modern motherboards, however, include a BIOS flashback feature that allows the BIOS to be updated or reloaded in any situation, even if the system doesn’t have a CPU.
  • Cooling a desktop computer is a complicated but important task. Modern CPUs and GPUs generate an enormous amount of heat (running at 70°C to 80°C under load with adequate cooling) that must be dissipated for the system to continue to run. There are two main types of modern CPU coolers. An air cooler typically features a very large heatsink (a mass of thermally conductive metal attached directly to the CPU), with fans blowing over it constantly to exhaust the heat it draws away from the CPU. A liquid cooler passes a coolant fluid over a cold plate in contact with the CPU to draw the heat into the liquid, then pumps the liquid through a radiator for heat exchange. A system will also include multiple case fans to maximize the flow of cool air into, and warm air out of, the case. Failing to adequately cool a system can result in thermal throttling, when a component intentionally lowers its performance level to avoid reaching and/or running at dangerous temperatures.
