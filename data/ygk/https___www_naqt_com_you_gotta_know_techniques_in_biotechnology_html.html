  • Current: Techniques in Biotechnology

You Gotta Know These Techniques in Biotechnology

  • PCR (polymerase chain reaction) is a technique used to amplify small segments of DNA. It allows scientists to generate millions of copies of a specific DNA sequence from a small initial sample. Developed by Kary Mullis in 1983, PCR involves repeated cycles of heating and cooling, performed using an instrument called a thermal cycler (or thermocycler). PCR is divided into three steps. First, denaturation heats a double-stranded DNA template, separating the two strands. Second, annealing lowers the temperature, allowing short single-stranded DNA strands called primers to bind to the template. Finally, extension allows DNA polymerase to extend the attached primers along the template. Taq polymerase, a DNA polymerase isolated from the bacteria Thermus aquaticus, is commonly used in PCR due to its heat stability. Variants of PCR include real-time PCR (or qPCR), which allows the amount of amplified product to be measured in real time, as well as reverse transcription PCR (or RT-PCR), which allows for quantification of RNA by converting RNA into complementary DNA prior to amplification.
  • Gel electrophoresis is used to separate nucleic acids or proteins based on their size and charge. It provides information on the size and quantity of DNA or protein fragments, enabling scientists to confirm the presence of specific genetic sequences or proteins. During gel electrophoresis, DNA, RNA, or protein samples are loaded into a gel matrix, such as agarose or polyacrylamide, and an electric current is applied. Molecules migrate through the gel, with smaller fragments moving faster than larger ones. During separation, buffers such as TAE (Tris-Acetate-EDTA) and TBE (Tris-Borate-EDTA) maintain pH, provide ions for conduction, and preserve sample stability. After separation, bands are visualized using staining methods such as ethidium bromide for nucleic acids or Coomassie brilliant blue for proteins. If the experiment is run alongside a set of molecules with known sizes called a ladder, the resulting bands may be compared against the ladder to determine the size of the fragments. SDS-PAGE is a variant of gel electrophoresis in which proteins are denatured using SDS (sodium dodecyl sulfate), conferring a uniform negative charge to all fragments and allowing for separation by size alone.
  • DNA sequencing is the process of determining the exact order of nucleotides within a DNA molecule. The first breakthrough in the field, Sanger sequencing, was developed by Frederick Sanger in 1977 and utilizes dideoxynucleotides (ddNTPs), which terminate DNA strand elongation when incorporated. A DNA sample is then subjected to denaturation, annealing, and elongation, with ddNTPs creating a set of fragments of varying lengths, each ending with a labeled ddNTP. In contrast, shotgun sequencing randomly fragments DNA strands, sequences those fragments into reads, then reassembles overlapping reads to recover the full sequence. Modern advancements in DNA sequencing include next-generation sequencing (or NGS) technologies, which allow for rapid, high-throughput sequencing of entire genomes. Industry leaders in next-generation sequencing include the US-based Illumina, who utilizes dye sequencing to facilitate massive parallel sequencing, as well as the UK-based Oxford Nanopore Technologies, whose nanopore sequencing method passes DNA samples through pores and measures changes in electric current across the pore.
  • Northern blotting is a technique used to detect and study specific RNA molecules within a sample. Developed by James Alwine, David Kemp, and George Stark in 1977, it allows scientists to determine the size, abundance, and expression patterns of RNA. The process begins with the extraction of RNA from a biological sample, followed by its separation by size through gel electrophoresis. The separated RNA fragments are then blotted onto a nylon or nitrocellulose membrane. To detect specific RNA sequences, the membrane is hybridized with a labeled probe that is complementary to the target RNA. After hybridization, the membrane is washed to remove unbound probes, and the signal is detected using autoradiography or a similar detection method. Northern blotting is widely used in gene expression studies, allowing researchers to compare RNA levels across different tissues or developmental stages. It is particularly valuable in identifying and quantifying mRNA levels, providing insights into gene regulation.
  • Western blotting is a powerful analytical technique used to detect specific proteins in a complex mixture. Developed by W. Neal Burnette in 1981, this method allows scientists to determine the presence, size, and abundance of proteins. The process begins with the extraction of proteins from a sample, followed by their separation based on size using SDS-PAGE. The separated proteins are then blotted onto a membrane, typically made of nitrocellulose or PVDF (polyvinylidene fluoride). To detect the target protein, the membrane is incubated with a primary antibody specific to the protein of interest. After washing away unbound primary antibodies, the membrane is incubated with a secondary antibody that recognizes the primary antibody and is conjugated to a detection enzyme or fluorescent tag. The presence of the target protein is then visualized using chemiluminescence, fluorescence, or colorimetric detection methods. Western blotting is widely used in molecular biology, biochemistry, and medical diagnostics to study protein expression, post-translational modifications, and protein-protein interactions.
  • Southern blotting is a method used to detect specific DNA sequences within a complex DNA sample. Developed by Edwin Southern in 1975, this technique enables researchers to identify, isolate, and study particular DNA fragments. The process begins with the extraction and restriction enzyme digestion of DNA. The resulting fragments are then separated by size using gel electrophoresis. The separated DNA is transferred onto a nylon or nitrocellulose membrane through capillary action, where the DNA fragments retain their relative positions. To detect specific sequences, the blotted membrane is hybridized with a labeled DNA probe complementary to the target sequence. After hybridization, the membrane is washed to remove unbound probes, and the signal is detected using autoradiography or a similar detection method. Southern blotting is commonly used in gene mapping, cloning verification, and genetic screening, providing valuable information about gene structure and organization as well as the presence of specific genetic elements.
  • ELISA (enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay) is a widely used technique for detecting and quantifying specific proteins, antibodies, antigens, and hormones in a sample. Developed in the 1970s, ELISA is based on the principle of antigen-antibody interaction. There are several types of ELISA, including direct, indirect, sandwich, and competitive, the most popular of which is sandwich ELISA. In a typical sandwich ELISA, the process begins by coating a microplate with a capture antibody specific to the target antigen. The sample is added, and any antigen present binds to the capture antibody. A detection antibody, which is also specific to the antigen and conjugated to an enzyme, is then added. After washing away unbound substances, a substrate for the enzyme is added, producing a measurable color change, fluorescence, or luminescence. The intensity of the signal correlates with the amount of target antigen in the sample. ELISA is highly sensitive and specific, making it a standard tool in clinical diagnostics, vaccine development, and research to measure biomarkers and disease-related molecules.
  • CRISPR/Cas9 is a revolutionary genome-editing technology derived from a bacterial immune system. It allows precise modification of DNA within living organisms. Discovered in the early 2010s by Jennifer Doudna and Emmanuelle Charpentier, the duo shared the 2020 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for their discovery. The system consists of two key components: the Cas9 enzyme and a guide RNA (gRNA). The gRNA is designed to match the target DNA sequence, guiding Cas9 to the specific location in the genome. Once there, Cas9 creates a double-strand break in the DNA. The cell’s natural repair mechanisms then kick in, either joining the ends back together through NHEJ (non-homologous end joining), which can introduce mutations, or through HDR (homology-directed repair), which uses a provided DNA template to insert new sequences. This precise targeting allows for gene knockout, insertion, or correction. In November 2018, CRISPR/Cas9 technology was controversially used by Chinese researcher He Jiankui to target the CCR5 gene in a pair of twins in an attempt to confer HIV resistance to them.
  • Flow cytometry is a technique used to analyze the physical and chemical characteristics of cells or particles as they pass in a stream through a laser. Developed in the 1960s, this method labels cells with fluorescent markers that bind to specific cell components, such as proteins or nucleic acids. Techniques such as hydrodynamic focusing and acoustic focusing ensure cells remain in single file as they pass through the beam. As cells move through the laser, light emitted by fluorescent markers—along with forward-scattered light (FSC light) and side-scattered light (SSC light)—are captured by detectors. The resulting data is analyzed using techniques such as compensation and gating to determine cell size, granularity, and the presence of specific markers. Flow cytometry is often combined with fluorescence in-situ hybridization (FISH) in the Flow-FISH technique, which is used to quantify the copy number of repetitive elements like telomeres. It is also widely used for fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS), as well as for immunophenotyping, cell counting, and biomarker detection.
  • Molecular cloning is a technique used to create multiple copies of a specific DNA segment. It involves the insertion of a DNA fragment of interest into a vector, a DNA molecule capable of replication in a host organism, usually a plasmid. The process begins with the isolation of the DNA fragment to be cloned. The isolated DNA is then fragmented using restriction enzymes, while the selected vector is digested using the same restriction enzyme. The DNA fragments and vectors are mixed in the presence of DNA ligase. The resulting recombinant vector is introduced into a host cell, often a bacterium, through a process called transformation. As the host cell replicates, it also replicates the recombinant vector, producing multiple copies of the DNA fragment. Often, an antibacterial resistance gene is incorporated into the vector, allowing only cells that have taken up the vector to survive. The cloned DNA can then be isolated and used for various applications, such as gene expression studies, genetic engineering, and functional analysis.
