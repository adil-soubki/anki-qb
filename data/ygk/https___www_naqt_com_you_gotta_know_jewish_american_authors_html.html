You Gotta Know These Jewish-American Authors

  • Isaac Asimov (1920–1992) is considered one of the “Big Three” science fiction writers alongside Arthur C. Clarke and Robert Heinlein. His Robot series (1940–1995), which includes 37 short stories and six novels, opens with the smaller collection I, Robot, which centers on the robopsychologist Dr. Susan Calvin and her accounts of her life’s work to reporters. The collection introduces his Three Laws of Robotics: robots may not injure a human or allow them to be harmed; robots must obey a human’s orders; and robots must protect their existence. Asimov’s other well-known works include the Foundation series (1942–1993), in which the fall of the human Galactic Empire is mitigated by the work of the “psychohistorian” Hari Seldon, and such short stories as “The Last Question” (1956) and “Nightfall” (1941).
  • Saul Bellow (1915–2005) was born to Jewish parents in Montreal and wrote many works about Jewish life, though he often rejected the label of “Jewish writer.” His novel Herzog (1964) centers on Moses E. Herzog, a divorced Jewish professor who debates killing his second wife, Madeleine, and her new husband, Valentine, to gain custody of his daughter Junie. In the picaresque novel The Adventures of Augie March (1953), Bellow wrote about a Chicago-born “everyman” who marries Stella, whom he meets in Mexico after teaching an eagle how to catch iguanas with Thea Fenchel. In Humboldt’s Gift (1975), Bellow fictionalized himself as Charlie Citrine, the protégé of the writer Von Humboldt Fleisher, a stand-in for Bellow’s own mentor, the Jewish poet Delmore Schwartz. At age 85, Bellow wrote Ravelstein (2000), in which the dying title Jewish man Abe Ravelstein tells his life story to the narrator, who catches a tropical disease in the Caribbean before deciding to write Ravelstein’s memoir.
  • Michael Chabon (1963–) has explored his Jewish identity in novels such as The Amazing Adventures of Kavalier & Clay (2000). The title Jewish cousins, Joe Kavalier and Sammy Klayman, work at Empire Comics and create a character called the Escapist, who is voiced by the fictional actor Tracy Bacon, as well as Luna Moth, based on the bohemian Rosa Saks. In the novel, Kavalier comes to the US to live with Sammy after he escapes Nazi-occupied Prague by hiding in a coffin with a golem. The Yiddish Policemen’s Union (2007) focuses on Meyer Landsman, who works with Berko Shemets in a Jewish refugee settlement in Alaska to investigate threats to blow up the Dome of the Rock. He also wrote The Final Solution (2004), which centers on a beekeeper and retired detective (implied to be Sherlock Holmes) who searches for the parrot of Jewish refugee Linus Steinman.
  • Emma Lazarus (1849–1887) was a Jewish author and activist best known for her sonnet “The New Colossus” (1883), which was written to raise money for the Statue of Liberty’s pedestal and is now inscribed on a plaque in the pedestal itself. The poem opens “not like the brazen giant of Greek fame,” describing how Liberty cries out “give me your tired, your poor, your huddled masses yearning to breathe free.” The poem, which was etched onto the statue’s pedestal, was inspired partly by her advocacy for Jewish refugees subject to persecution in Russia. Lazarus also wrote “In the Jewish Synagogue at Newport” (1871) as a rebuttal to Henry Wadsworth Longfellow’s “The Jewish Cemetery at Newport.”
  • Philip Roth (1933–2018) created the fictional Jewish character Nathan Zuckerman, often considered Roth’s alter ego, who appears in novels such as American Pastoral (1997). The novel focuses on Seymour “The Swede” Levov, who runs a glove factory in Newark and has a daughter who converts to Jainism after she bombs a post office to protest the Vietnam War. In Portnoy’s Complaint (1969), Roth wrote about the “nice Jewish boy” Alexander Portnoy, who monologues to his psychoanalyst Dr. Spielvogel about his parents Sophie and Jack, as well as his relationships with women he nicknames “The Monkey” (Mary Jane Reed) and “The Pumpkin.” Roth’s The Human Stain (2000), also narrated by Nathan Zuckerman, is about the Classics professor Coleman Silk, who was fired for using a racial slur, even though he is a Black man passing as white. Roth explored Jewish identity in short stories such as “The Conversion of the Jews” and “Defender of the Faith” in his short story collection Goodbye, Columbus (1959), which shares its title with a novella about Neil Klugman. Rising antisemitism forms the backdrop of Roth’s The Plot Against America (2004), an alternative history novel in which Charles Lindbergh defeats Franklin D. Roosevelt for president, leading to the increased persecution of Jews.
  • Isaac Bashevis Singer (1904–1991) was a Polish-born American short story author who wrote many works in Yiddish, drawing from the writings of earlier Yiddish authors such as Sholem Aleichem. Singer, who emigrated to the US in 1935 and worked for the Jewish Daily Forward, is perhaps best known for “Gimpel the Fool” (1953), in which the title breadmaker marries the unfaithful Elka and is told by the devil to urinate in the village’s bread to get revenge on his neighbors. “Gimpel the Fool,” like “The Spinoza of Market Street” and many more of Singer’s stories, is set in a fictionalized version of the Polish town of Frampol. His story “Yentl, the Yeshiva Boy,” about a young woman who disguises herself as a boy to study at a yeshiva, was adapted into a 1983 film starring Barbra Streisand. Singer also wrote the novel Satan in Goray (1933), about the title village amid the Khmelnitsky Uprising in Poland.
  • Bernard Malamud (1914–1986) was a Brooklyn-born author best known for his novel The Natural (1952). The novel centers on the baseball player Roy Hobbs, who tragically splits his prized bat “Wonderboy” in half. Hobbs plays for the New York Knights, which is managed by Pop Fisher. In the novel, Hobbs is shot by Harriet Byrd with a silver bullet, leading him to retire for 15 years, though during this time, he longs for Iris Lemon despite dating Memo Paris. Malamud more directly explored Judaism and antisemitism in his novel The Fixer (1966), an adaptation of the real-life story of Menahem Mendel Beilis, in which the handyman Yakov Bok is falsely accused of ritually killing a Christian boy during Passover and is tortured in tsarist Russia. Malamud is also known for “The Jewbird” (1963), a short story about a crow named Schwartz who endures antisemitism.
  • Chaim Potok (1929–2002) was an author and rabbi who sold over 3.4 million copies of his novel The Chosen (1967), which centers on the Hasidic boy Danny Saunders and the Orthodox boy Reuven Malter. The novel follows Danny’s decision to become a Freudian psychologist against the wishes of his rabbi father, while Reuven becomes a rabbi years after he is hit in the eye by a baseball. Potok’s other novels include My Name is Asher Lev (1972), whose title character distresses his traditionalist Hasidic father Aryeh by studying painting under Jacob Kahn and depicting his mother Rivkeh in his painting “Brooklyn Crucifixion,” and The Promise (1969), a sequel to The Chosen that centers Reuven and Danny amid post-World War II New York.
  • Arthur Miller (1915–2005) was born to a Polish-Jewish family in Harlem and achieved success with plays such as Death of a Salesman (1949) about the decline of Willy Loman, who crashes his car to provide insurance money for his wife Linda and his sons Biff and Happy after he is fired from his job and becomes obsessed with his own dreams. Miller’s play The Crucible (1953) is a depiction of the Salem witch trials written as an allegory for McCarthyism, in which John Proctor and Giles Corey are among those executed after Abigail Williams accuses many citizens of Salem of participating in witchcraft. Miller’s All My Sons (1947) is about Joe Keller, whose decision to sell faulty airplane pilots to the Air Force during World War II causes the imprisonment of his partner Steve Deever, the deaths of 21 pilots, and the suicide of his son Larry. After the Fall (1964), which is set entirely within the mind of its protagonist Quentin, was directly inspired by Miller’s divorce from Marilyn Monroe.
  • Neil Simon (1927–2018) was a Jewish-American playwright from New York City who grew up during the Great Depression, which framed some of his plays. Simon is best known for The Odd Couple (1965), which depicts the lazy Oscar Madison and his neat roommate Felix Ungar, who go on a disastrous double date with Cecily and Gwendolyn Pigeon. Simon won the 1991 Pulitzer Prize for Drama for Lost in Yonkers (1990), in which Arty and Jay Kurnitz live with their mentally challenged Aunt Bella and attempt to steal money from their grandmother, a store owner. Simon’s semi-autobiographical Eugene trilogy tells the story of Eugene Jerome, whose New York childhood, World War II basic training, and early comedy career are respectively chronicled in Brighton Beach Memoirs (1983), Biloxi Blues (1985), and Broadway Bound (1986).

This article was contributed by NAQT writer Noah Sheidlower.
