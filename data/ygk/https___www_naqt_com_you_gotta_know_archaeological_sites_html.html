You Gotta Know These Archaeological Sites

  • Lascaux (France) In 1940 French teenager Marcel Ravidat’s dog found a hole under a recently fallen tree that led to a totally unknown cave system rich with prehistoric cave paintings. He initially thought it was a rumored secret tunnel to nearby Lascaux Manor. Lascaux’s trove of cave art comprises some 600 paintings; some of the most famous are in the Hall of the Bulls, where many panels depict extinct bovines called aurochs—some stretching over 16 feet in length. A passage off the Hall of the Bulls leads to chambers called the Apse, Nave, and Shaft; the Nave’s panels include a scene of deer swimming, while the Shaft hosts a much-studied image of a bison next to a fallen, naked, bird-headed man. Since 1963, touring Lascaux has been tightly restricted to limit damage from mold and humidity caused by visitors’s breath.
  • Mohenjo-daro (Pakistan) Located in central Pakistan, Mohenjo-daro was a sprawling city of the Indus Valley Civilization (once called the Harappan Civilization, after another of its type-sites, Harappa). It flourished in the late 3rd millennium BC; many scholars hold that regular “mud floods” and depletion of nearby natural resources caused its gradual abandonment. Unlike most early cities, Mohenjo-daro bears evidence of urban design and planning: it was built on a deliberate grid containing multiple “islands” of uniform rectangular dimensions. A central “Citadel” looms higher than the rest of the city and contained a huge granary and a sunken brick Great Bath. Two artifacts from Mohenjo-daro have become iconic: the “Dancing Girl,” a four-inch bronze statuette of a girl posed with one hand on her hip; and the speculatively titled “Priest-King,” a soapstone sculpture of an imperious, bearded man wearing a floral print robe.
  • Ur (Iraq) The ancient ruins near the modern-day Iraqi town of Tell el-Muqayya were known for centuries, but it was only in the mid-19th century that Henry Rawlinson identified them as Ur, a Sumerian city-state that thrived in the 3rd millennium BC. The Great Ziggurat of Ur, dedicated to Ur’s patron deity Nanna, was identified relatively early; the other major artifacts at Ur were uncovered by British Museum projects led by Sir Leonard Woolley, particularly at Ur’s Royal Cemetery. Notable artifacts include the Standard of Ur, a wooden box inlaid with three tiers of mosaics showing processions of soldiers and animals; the Lyres of Ur, a pair of harp-like instruments stylized with bulls’ heads; and a lapis lazuli cylinder seal identifying the lavish tomb of “Queen” Puabi.
  • Knossos (Greece) Knossos contains ruins of the Minoans, a Bronze Age society on the Greek island of Crete. Excavations began in 1877, but it was the English archaeologist Sir Arthur Evans’s projects starting in 1900 that unearthed its most significant finds, including much of the Palace of Minos. The palace’s Throne Room has walls painted in tiers of red and white, with images of animals and foliage; elsewhere, an iconic “bull-leaping fresco” shows the Minoans’ daredevil tradition of jumping over bulls. Two writing systems identified at Knossos were grouped as Linear A and Linear B; Linear A remains undeciphered to this day. Evans also led reconstructions at Knossos now regarded as dubious in their accuracy, particularly his assembling of so-called “snake goddess” figurines of women holding wriggling snakes.
  • Luxor and the Valley of the Kings (Egypt) The modern-day city of Luxor sits over what was once the Egyptian capital Thebes, which itself lay across the Nile from its necropolis, the Valley of the Kings. The sheer density of ancient archaeological sites near Luxor has led some to liken it to one huge open-air museum. Luxor is home to the Luxor Temple, whose entry is flanked with twin statues of Ramses II. The Avenue of the Sphinxes links Luxor Temple to Karnak, another huge temple noted for its towering Great Hypostyle Hall, whose columns are inscribed top-to-bottom with hieroglyphs. The nearby Valley of the Kings is home to many royal burial sites, all designated “KV.” Such sites include KV62, where Howard Carter located the tomb of King Tutankhamun, and KV20, the lavish long-ramped mortuary temple of Hatshepsut, a powerful female pharaoh.
  • Mausoleum of Qin Shi Huang (China) Qin Shi Huang, the first emperor of a unified China, was buried in the 3rd century BC in a mausoleum near the city of Xi’an. His tomb is under an extremely broad truncated earthen pyramid, “protected” by the Terracotta Army, a collection of over 8,000 life-size clay statues of soldiers, cavalry, archers, and officers, all facing east (the direction of the emperor’s rivals). Weapons buried near the army were made using an unusual thirteen-metal alloy and were still sharp when disinterred. Little of the tomb itself, discovered in the 1970s, has yet been excavated, though two grand bronze four-horsed chariots were dug up in the 1980s. High levels of mercury contamination in the ground may confirm an account in Sima Qian’s Annals of the Grand Historian claiming that the tomb boasted artificial rivers of flowing mercury.
  • Pompeii (Italy) The sudden eruption of Mount Vesuvius in AD 79 buried the Roman town of Pompeii (and nearby Herculanaeum) so completely and quickly that the towns remained remarkably intact until they were unearthed in modern times. Notable sites in Pompeii include the House of the Faun, whose Alexander Mural features a much-reproduced depiction of Alexander the Great; the Villa of the Mysteries, whose red murals depict a cult initiation ceremony; the Lupanar, a brothel where graffiti survives intact on its walls; and the Garden of the Fugitives, where plaster casts preserve the poses of human remains found there, as if frozen in time by the eruption.
  • Sutton Hoo (England) Sutton Hoo, an area near the North Sea coast in eastern England, is a complex of two cemeteries and numerous burial mounds (or tumuli) discovered in 1938 that provide insight to the early Anglo-Saxon settlers of England. Sutton Hoo’s ship burial yielded a cache of grave goods that invited many comparisons to passages in Beowulf. While the interred ship had long since decayed, it left behind a uniform negative impression of colored soil, allowing scholars to determine its size and shape. The ship burial may have honored the East Anglian king Raedwald, but this theory is not universally accepted. Among the site’s finest treasures are intricate, bejeweled garnet-and-gold clasps and buckles for clothes and satchels, including a precious purse lid. An icon of the site itself is the iron-and-bronze Sutton Hoo helmet, which is topped with a small dragon figurine and has a face mask adorned with eyebrows and a mustache.
  • Chichén Itzá (Mexico) The Mayan city of Chichén Itzá was established in the 6th century AD on the Yucatán Peninsula. Structures such as the Akabtzib (“House of Dark Writing”) were built by the city’s original settlers, who were displaced by invaders around the 10th century during the much-debated “post-classical Mayan collapse.” These invaders built the city’s Temple of Kukulkan (also known as El Castillo), a grand Mesoamerican pyramid whose four sides each have 91-step stairways (which, when including the shared top platform, sum to 365 steps, one for each day of the year). Chichén Itzá also hosts a court for the Mesoamerican ballgame decorated with low-relief images of human sacrifices. Sacrifice also occurred in the city’s largest cenote (natural underground well), with people (and jade treasures) cast into it to appease the rain god Chaac.
  • L’Anse Aux Meadows (Canada) Rediscovered at the very northern tip of Newfoundland in the 1960s, L’Anse aux Meadows serves as concrete proof that Norse seafarers were the first Europeans to reach North America (excluding Greenland). The site, established around AD 1000, may in fact be Straumfjord, a town built in “Vinland” according to the Saga of Erik the Red. The presence of many rivets strongly suggests that it, at least in part, was a ship repair depot, and the presence of butternut seeds proves the Norse traveled at least to New Brunswick or met native traders from there. The main edifices at L’Anse aux Meadows were built from sod, with dirt walls and turf roofs. Its foundry was where the earliest known iron-working occurred in the New World.
